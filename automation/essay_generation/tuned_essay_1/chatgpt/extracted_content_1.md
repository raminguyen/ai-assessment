Each morning, Professor Catlove opens a fresh can of cat food, and immediately his cats run into the kitchen, purring, meowing, and rubbing against his legs. ## **Classical Conditioning in the Cats’ Behavior**
Classical conditioning occurs when a neutral stimulus becomes associated with an unconditioned stimulus through repeated pairings, eventually eliciting a conditioned response. In this case, the sound and sight of Professor Catlove opening a can originally had no inherent meaning for the cats. Over time, however, the metallic click of the can opener, the tearing of the lid, and the professor’s early-morning kitchen routine have become conditioned stimuli. They reliably predict the arrival of food—the unconditioned stimulus—which naturally elicits salivation, excitement, and approach behaviors.
As a result of these repeated associations, the conditioned stimuli alone are now sufficient to trigger the cats’ rush to the kitchen and their vocalizations and affectionate behaviors. Numerous studies have demonstrated that animals readily form such auditory-food associations; for instance, the dogs in Pavlov’s classic experiments began salivating at the sound of a bell because it consistently preceded feeding. Similarly, the cats’ meowing and purring upon hearing the can open function as conditioned responses. Even their physical contact—rubbing against the professor—may be partly a conditioned response, since affectionate rubbing commonly precedes successful feeding episodes and has been reinforced through repetition.
This example also illustrates stimulus generalization. It is possible that any sound resembling a can opening, such as a soda can being popped, might produce similar responses in the cats. Many pet owners report this phenomenon, and research has shown that animals often respond to similar but not identical cues once conditioning has taken place. Thus, in this morning ritual, classical conditioning is clearly shaping the cats' anticipatory behaviors.
## **Operant Conditioning in the Cats’ Behavior**
Operant conditioning involves learning through consequences—behaviors that lead to desirable outcomes are more likely to be repeated. The cats’ actions in the kitchen can also be interpreted through this framework. Purring, meowing, and leg-rubbing may initially occur naturally, but when these behaviors consistently precede the delivery of food, they become reinforced. Food acts as a powerful positive reinforcer, and the cats learn that engaging with Professor Catlove increases the likelihood of receiving it quickly.
In many households, cats who vocalize loudly or insistently during feeding routines often receive food sooner, unintentionally teaching them that persistence pays off. If Professor Catlove has ever rewarded particularly eager meowing by feeding the cats immediately, operant conditioning would further strengthen those behaviors. Over time, the cats’ enthusiastic greeting becomes a learned routine shaped by the contingencies of reinforcement.
In addition to positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement may play a role for the professor: feeding the cats may stop their noisy demands, effectively removing an unpleasant stimulus. This consequence makes the professor more likely to feed them promptly in the future, demonstrating how operant conditioning can shape human behavior as well.
## **Classical Conditioning in the Professor’s Behavior**
Although humans often view themselves as the trainers rather than the trained, Professor Catlove may also show behavior shaped by classical conditioning. Over time, the sound and action of opening the can may become associated with these pleasant emotional responses. In this sense, the professor may be conditioned to enjoy or anticipate the morning ritual because of the positive associations he has developed with the cats’ reactions.
This dynamic is not unusual; research shows that humans can form conditioned emotional responses in everyday contexts, often without conscious awareness. The professor may therefore experience positive affect when he engages in the feeding routine because the cats’ affectionate behaviors—initially unconditioned positive stimuli—have become paired with the feeding process itself.

If the professor enjoys these behaviors, he may be more likely to continue or even increase his consistent feeding routine. Many pet–owner relationships involve this reciprocal reinforcement cycle, in which both parties reward one another through predictable interactions.
## **Social Learning in the Cats and the Professor**
Social learning—the process of acquiring behaviors by observing others—may play subtler roles in the morning scene. Studies show that animals, including cats, can learn feeding behaviors, avoidance behaviors, and social signals by observing conspecifics.
